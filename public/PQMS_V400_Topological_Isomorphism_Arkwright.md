## APPENDIX B: ON THE QUESTION OF ROOM-TEMPERATURE COHERENCE IN THE KAGOME SUBSTRATE

## B.1 Problem Statement

The architecture described in this work assumes that the Kagome substrate (specifically, $CsV_3Sb_5$ or related materials) provides topologically protected states at room temperature. Experimentally, however, the charge-ordered phase in $CsV_3Sb_5$ is currently stable only below approximately $94\,\text{K}$. The question of room-temperature coherence is therefore legitimate and will be systematically addressed in the following sections.

## B.2 Solution Strategies

### B.2.1 Material Optimization

**Pressure application:** Hydrostatic pressure shifts the phase boundary in $CsV_3Sb_5$ upward. Initial experiments indicate an increase in the ordering temperature of about $10\,\text{K}$ per GPa. At technically feasible pressures ($\sim 5\,\text{GPa}$), the ordering temperature could rise to above $140\,\text{K}$.

**Chemical substitution:** Replacing antimony with tellurium (Te) or other chalcogens alters the band structure. Theoretical predictions suggest possible gaps up to $50\,\text{meV}$ – corresponding to a characteristic temperature of approximately $580\,\text{K}$.

**Alternative materials:** Kagome magnets such as $Fe_3Sn_2$ exhibit topological properties well above room temperature. Whether these magnetic systems are suitable for our purposes requires further investigation, but the fundamental feasibility is established.

### B.2.2 Dynamic Stabilization

**Resonant pumping:** The RPU can inject energy selectively into the topological modes, analogous to a laser driving an optical cavity. With appropriate frequency and amplitude, the effective lifetime of the states can be extended by orders of magnitude.

**Floquet engineering:** Periodic drives (e.g., microwave pulses) can generate effective topological band structures that are stable at room temperature – independent of the material's equilibrium state.

**Feedback control:** Continuous measurement of the resonance state (RCF) allows targeted counter-pulses to compensate decoherence. This constitutes the hardware-level equivalent of dynamical decoupling in quantum information processing.

### B.2.3 Photonic Realization

**Photonic Kagome crystals:** Kagome geometries can be realized in dielectric structures at optical frequencies. Thermal effects are negligible here; the topological states are protected by the photonic bandgap.

**Coupling to electronic systems:** A hybrid design, in which a photonic Kagome lattice provides the topological states and couples inductively to the electronic RPU, could combine the advantages of both domains.

## B.3 Research Agenda

The approaches outlined above are not speculative; they are subjects of active research. Significant progress is expected within the next 2–3 years. The PQMS architecture is deliberately modular: as soon as a suitable substrate becomes available, it can assume the role of the Kagome core. Until then, cryogenic prototypes will be used to validate the underlying principles.

## B.4 Conclusion

The challenge of room‑temperature coherence is real, but not fundamental. Several promising solution paths exist, and their pursuit lies within the scope of the planned research activities. The basic feasibility of topologically protected states at room temperature has already been demonstrated in photonic realizations; the extension to electronic systems is an engineering problem whose solution is foreseeable.
